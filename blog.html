Rice farming in the Philippines is a labor-intensive and often backbreaking job. Farmers typically work long hours, from dawn to dusk, and they use traditional methods of cultivation, such as hand-transplanting rice seedlings and harvesting rice by hand. Despite the hard work, rice farming is a way of life for many Filipinos, and it has been passed down from generation to generation.

Corn farmers in the Philippines play a crucial role in the country's agricultural landscape, contributing significantly to food security and the economy. Corn, also known as maize, is the second most important staple crop in the Philippines after rice, serving as a primary source of nutrition for millions of Filipinos. Corn is also a versatile crop with diverse uses, including animal feed, industrial processing, and biofuel production.

The Philippines is one of the world's leading mango producers, and the Carabao mango, a native variety, is particularly renowned for its sweet and juicy flavor. Mango farming is a labor-intensive process that requires careful attention to detail, from planting and pruning to pest and disease control. Mango farmers typically work long hours in the sun, and the work can be physically demanding.

Tubo farmers are the people who are responsible for cultivating and harvesting sugarcane, a major crop in the Philippines. Sugarcane is used to produce sugar, molasses, and ethanol, and it is also a source of fiber for a variety of products. There are an estimated 1.5 million tubo farmers in the Philippines, and they account for about 10% of the country's labor force. Tubo farming is a labor-intensive process that requires careful attention to detail, from planting and harvesting to pest and disease control.
